# Social Media Usage and Mental Health

# Overview
This project explores how social media usage relates to mental health and well-being. Using a dataset on social media behavior and lifestyle factors, we examined relationships between screen time, social media platform preference, exercise habits, sleep quality, and happiness levels.

The goal of the analysis was to understand whether different patterns of social media use are associated with changes in stress, sleep quality, exercise frequency, and overall happiness.

# Research Questions
1. Does stress level differ across primary social media platforms?
2. Does exercise frequency differ based on the number of days without social media?
3. Do social media platform and age influence sleep quality?
4. Is there a relationship between daily screen time and happiness levels?

# Methods
Statistical analysis was conducted using the following methods:

- One-way ANOVA (stress level across social media platforms)
- Simple linear regression (exercise frequency vs. days without social media)
- Two-way ANOVA / Type II ANOVA (sleep quality vs. platform and age)
- ANOVA with Tukey post-hoc test (happiness vs. screen time levels)

# Key Findings
Most variables did not show significant relationships with mental health outcomes. Social media platform choice, age, and days without social media were not strong predictors of stress, sleep quality, or exercise frequency.

However, **daily screen time showed a strong and statistically significant relationship with happiness levels**. Individuals with high screen time (5+ hours per day) had significantly lower happiness scores compared to low or moderate users.

This suggests that the amount of screen time, rather than the specific platform used, may have a stronger association with overall well-being.
